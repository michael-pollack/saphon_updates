Synthesis Notes

Given the available data, it is not possible to come up with a single analysis of nasal-oral allophony/allomorphy that accounts for all of the observed data of the language. We posit a series of nasal consonants /m, n, ŋ/ with a series of allophones: [ND, D, DN, N]. [DN] allophones only in word-final position immediately following an oral vowel, e.g. /apuan/ [apuadn] 'hitting'. [D] allophones occur in word-initial position before an oral vowel, and in word-medial position between two oral vowels (e.g. [pudaki] 'eel'). [N] allophones occur in word-initial before an nasal vowel, in word-medial position between two nasal vowels, and in word-final position after a nasal vowel. [ND] allophones occur in word-medial position, between a nasal and an oral vowel. Some [D]s which appear at the beginning of a root when the root lacks any prefixes are realized as [ND] when a prefix with a nasal vowel attaches to its left. However, stating that these prefixes have underlying nasal vowels is problematic, as the prefixes do not always suface as nasal (see MPP:prefix). Sequences of the type [N... ND], [N ... N], [ND ... DN] are observed, but sequences of the type [DN ... ND] and [ND ... N] are not observed. It is unclear whether this language exhibits LDNH: In some cases, it seems likely, but in other cases, it seems untenable. If the language does exhibit a process of LDNH, it may only be triggered by a root-final nasal vowel, only spreading to vowels and approximants and blocked by all other segments, such that it is restricted in its productivity.

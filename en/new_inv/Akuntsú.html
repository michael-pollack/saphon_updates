Synthesis Notes

We adopt Aragon's (2014) analysis of Akuntsú. Following this analysis, Akuntsú presents a series of 3 nasal consonants /m, n, ŋ/ that contrast with voiced obstruents /b, d, ɡ/ as well as five vowels which contrast in their oral and nasal qualities. We assume five phonological processes: (1) rightward LDNH, the right-most nasal vowel of a root triggers optional nasalization of oral vowels and /ɾ, w, j/. This process is blocked by any obstruent (T - voiceless obstruent, D - voiced obstruent); (2) bidirectional LN, in an unstressed syllable oral vowels and /w, j/ optionall nasalize before or after a nasal consonant or nasal vowel; (3) rightward BN, nasalization of /b/ to [m] root-initially; (4) leftward LN, nasalization of /b/ to [m] before a nasal vowel; (5) leftward LN, morpheme-final /t/ nasalizes to [n] before a morpheme-initial nasal vowel. We consider leftward LDNH and bidirectional LN to be optional in Akuntsú, based on the following facts: (1) transcriptions of the same word show both forms where all targets of LDNH surface as nasal and ones in which only some of the targets surface as as nasal, e.g. for LDNH, /aɾamĩra/ [a.ɾa.'mĩɾ̃ɐ̃] ~ [ɐ̃.nɐ̃.'mĩɾ̃ɐ̃] 'woman', /kɨrẽ/ [kɨ.ɾ̃ẽ] ~ [kə̃.nɛ̃] 'today' (Aragon, 2014:48), and for LN, /ameko/ [ɔ.mɛ̃ŋ.'ku] ~ [ɔ.mɛ̃.'ku] 'jaguar', /peniket/ [pe.nĩŋ.'kʲɛt̚] ~ [pe.nĩ.kʲɛt̚] 'ladybug (sp.)', /o=ø-mepit/ [o.mɛ̃m.'bit̚] ~ [omɛ̃.'bit̚] 'my son' (Aragon, 2014:46); (2) the phrasing in Aragon's analysis suggests that such processes are not obligatory in Akuntsú: "In Akuntsú, for example, sonorants are most likely to have the nasality spread to them, as they can also be affected by nasality; obstruents tend to block any tentative spreading of the [+nasal] feature to the preceding vowel, though stops may be affected by nasality" (Aragon, 2014:48) and "That is, in unstressed syllables, vowels tend to become nasalized (showing up on the surface as phonetic nasal vowels) as a result of progressive or regressive assimilation when adjacent to a [+nasal] segment, either a nasal consonant or a nasal vowel. Vowels that are underlyingly oral in stressed position adjacent by a nasal segment do not become nasalized through the spreading of the [+nasal] feature of the adjacent nasal segment" (Aragon, 2014:45). Following Aragon we assume that laryngealized oral and nasal vowels are stress-conditioned, only occuring when they appear in a stressed syllable. We interpret Aragon as saying that the vowel lengthening processess, which occurs in stressed syllables, is optional, based on the following observation: "On the basis of these findings, vowels in stressed syllables tend to have longer duration than those in non-stressed syllables" (p. 57). We also interpret the phenomenon captured by the following rule ∅ → C[+long]/VC_V́ to be consonant lengthening in an intervocalic environment, rather than epenthesis.
